Responsibility Determines Scale
Why We See Different Problems

An unfinished note on leadership, communication, and innovation.

On a Friday afternoon, after spending nearly a year developing a proposal for infrastructure innovation, I finally had thirty minutes to present it.

It wasn't a large audience—just an internal meeting with colleagues and managers from different branches.

I spoke with everything I had. I explained the workflow bottlenecks, the architecture, the pilot, and the long-term vision.

When I finished, I looked around the room.

Nothing.

No excitement.

No resistance.

No curiosity.

Only the same calm expressions I had seen a year earlier when I first shared the idea.

After the meeting, I drove to the American River Parkway and walked for nearly an hour.

I wasn't asking why the proposal had failed.

I was asking a different question.

How could we look at the same proposal, yet see entirely different things?

It would have been easy to blame bureaucracy. Bureaucracy certainly mattered. But the longer I walked, the less convinced I became that it was the deepest explanation.

Instead, I began to suspect that the real difference was scale.

My proposal was asking a root question:

Why is the system designed this way in the first place?

The people around me were asking different questions:

Will this affect our current workflow?
How much will it cost?
Do we have the staff?
Can we deliver this on time?

None of these questions were wrong.

They simply belonged to different scales.

Over time I realized something important.

People are not necessarily looking at the same problem and reaching different conclusions.

They are often looking at different scales of the same reality.

A supervisor sees a team.

A branch manager sees a branch.

An executive sees an organization.

Responsibility expands some horizons while inevitably narrowing others.

Responsibility determines scale.

Scale determines what becomes visible.

That realization changed the way I communicate.

I used to believe that when people didn't understand an idea, I simply needed to provide more context.

Now I think the challenge is different.

Context explains.

Scale aligns.

Before discussing solutions, I first try to align the scale from which we are observing the problem.

Innovation is rarely rejected because people lack intelligence. More often, it is because they are looking from different scales of responsibility. Before changing minds, we must first align scales.
